# Knee injury exercise guidance

People recovering from ACL reconstruction should avoid deep loaded knee flexion (below 90 degrees) such as deep barbell back squats or heavy leg press for at least the first 4-6 months post-surgery, and should get clearance from a physical therapist before resuming.

Box squats or squats to a bench limiting depth to roughly parallel are generally lower-risk alternatives for people with prior knee injuries, since they reduce shear force at the knee joint compared to deep squats.

High-impact plyometric exercises (box jumps, jump squats, bounding) are generally contraindicated for people with active knee pain, patellar tendinopathy, or meniscus tears until pain-free range of motion is restored.

Leg extensions under heavy load can increase anterior knee stress and are often discouraged for people with patellofemoral pain syndrome ("runner's knee"); closed-chain exercises like step-ups or wall sits are often better tolerated.

Low-impact alternatives such as stationary cycling, swimming, and controlled bodyweight step-ups are generally considered safer starting points for people easing back into training after a knee injury.
